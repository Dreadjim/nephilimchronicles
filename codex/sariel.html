Sariel

The Moon-Commander · Keeper of Sinning Souls · Who Stands Between Holy and Fallen

Overview

NAME
Sariel / Sâraqâ'êl / Saraqael / Suriel / Juriel — Hebrew root disputed between "Prince of God," "Moon of God," and "Command of God." The Ethiopic Enoch tradition stabilises it as Saraqael. The Dead Sea Scrolls War Scroll (1QM) names him as one of the four archangels inscribed on the shields of the holy army.
RANK
One of the Seven Holy Angels of the Presence (1 Enoch 20:1-8) · Named on angelic shields in the DSS War Scroll (1QM 9:15-16) · Listed in some Watcher descent traditions as the 20th chief of the 200
SOURCE TEXT
1 Enoch 20:6 (primary attribution) · Dead Sea Scrolls 1QM 9:15-16 · Book of Jubilees 1:27-2:2 · 3 Enoch 14 (Suriel variant) · Some manuscripts of 1 Enoch 10:1 attribute the warning to Noah to Sariel rather than Uriel

Physical Form

Ancient description does not freeze Sariel into a single form — it describes him as a quality of light rather than a fixed body. Where Shamsiel radiates solar heat that scorches the edge of perception, Sariel emanates something colder and more patient: the specific white of the moon when it is full and the night beneath it is utterly still. His aspect carries the phases — in his full presence, sources suggest he appears complete and luminous; in diminished presence, sectioned, part of his form withheld the way the moon withholds itself. The DSS War Scroll places his name on the fourth shield of the holy army alongside Michael, Gabriel, and Sariel — which means whatever form he carries into battle must be formidable enough to stand beside the Archangel of War.

The Specific Authority

1 Enoch 20:6 is precise and disturbing: "Saraqael, one of the holy angels, who is set over the spirits, who sin in the spirit." This is not the same as being set over sinners. It is being set over a specific category of transgression — spiritual sin. Not the violations of the body (metallurgy, weapons, cosmetics, written knowledge — the provinces of Azazel, Penemue, and the others) but the violations of the spirit. The internal corruptions. The apostasies of thought, of covenant, of sacred loyalty. Sariel presides over the accounting of inner betrayals. He is the angel most closely connected to what a soul has done with its immaterial self — and what must happen to that self after the body ends.

The Dual Nature Problem

Sariel represents one of the Enochic tradition's most unresolved tensions. He is listed among the seven holy archangels in 1 Enoch 20 — the same list that includes Michael, Raphael, Uriel, and Gabriel, the holy pillars of divine administration. Yet some traditions within the Watcher literature name him as one of the 200 who descended. The manuscripts disagree. The tension may be intentional: the Enochic tradition is not always interested in clean resolutions. What is established is that his domain — the moon, the seasons, the interior sins of spiritual beings — makes him uniquely positioned to understand the Watchers' transgression from the inside. He is the archangel who most directly presides over the kind of corruption the Watchers committed. Whether he fell into the category he governs, or governs it because he has always stood above it, is a question the ancient texts leave deliberately open.

Context

The seven-archangel schema in 1 Enoch 20 represents one of the most significant theological contributions of the Second Temple period: a structured celestial administration with specific portfolios. Sariel's portfolio — the sinning spirits — places him at the theological intersection of judgment, accountability, and the question of what happens to a fallen or corrupted spiritual being. This is the most uncomfortable portfolio in the archangel schema. Michael fights. Raphael heals. Uriel illuminates. Sariel decides what to do with beings who have failed in the spirit — including, implicitly, the Watchers who fell.

Cultural Echoes

Mesopotamian

Nanna/Sin — the moon god who weighs the hearts of the dead, whose light illuminates what darkness hides and whose cycles mark the sacred calendar

Egyptian

Thoth's lunar aspect — the divine accountant who records spiritual transgressions and weighs the hidden moral weight of souls in the Hall of Two Truths

Persian

Mazda's yazata of the moon — Mah, who records oaths and covenants and witnesses their keeping or breaking; the celestial notary of spiritual contracts

Kabbalistic

The Sephira Yesod — the lunar sphere, foundation of divine creative force, gateway between the higher divine light and the material world; the seat of the covenant

Theological Significance

Sariel is the answer to an unasked question: what happens to the Watchers' souls? Not their bodies — those are bound in Dudael, suspended in fire, imprisoned until the great day. But their immaterial selves, the spiritual substance that makes them what they are rather than what they do? Sariel is set over exactly that. Over the spirits who sin in the spirit. The Watchers descended in the body and transgressed in the spirit — not merely out of appetite but out of fundamental covenantal violation, choosing to abandon their designated station not because they were tempted by the body but because their inner nature, their spiritual will, turned away. Sariel presides over exactly that category of being. The moon watches everything. And the Moon-Commander remembers what it sees.

Active & Passive Abilities

LUNAR DECREECelestial · Permanent

Sariel commands the moon's phases and the seasonal calendar. He can accelerate or withhold the moon's light, pulling tides out of alignment and breaking the sacred timing of lunar festivals, covenants, and oaths sworn by moonlight. Calendrical disruption on the ground becomes spiritual chaos in the mortal order.

ActiveCelestial-Scale
SOUL REGISTRYPassive · Always On

Sariel has permanent awareness of the spiritual state of any being in his vicinity. He reads the interior sins — not the acts but the will behind the acts. Characters who have made covenantal betrayals, apostasies of loyalty, or hidden spiritual compromises are immediately visible to him as if lit from within. There is no concealment of spiritual condition in his presence.

PassiveDetection
JUDGMENT MARKTouch · Instant

Sariel marks a soul for the specific accounting that follows death. A mortal marked by Sariel carries an invisible notation — visible to other celestial beings — that they are flagged for the deepest spiritual review. This does not kill. It makes death significantly more consequential when it comes. Every spiritual authority in the post-mortem review will see the mark.

ActivePermanent
SILVER SUSPENSION30 ft · Concentration

Sariel can suspend the spiritual component of a being in a state of arrested judgment — not dead, not free, held in the lunar stillness between one breath and the next. A being held in Silver Suspension experiences complete awareness without the ability to act spiritually. They watch their own state being assessed. Duration: until Sariel ends it or is interrupted.

ActiveCrowd Control

⚠ Known Vulnerabilities & Counters

  • His authority is specifically over spiritual sin. Beings who have committed only physical transgressions with entirely pure spiritual intent — true warriors, those acting under coercion — register differently in his assessment. He cannot mark what has not spiritually consented to its transgression.
  • His dual-nature status creates a theological vulnerability: invoking his ambiguity — addressing him as both archangel and fallen — can destabilise his authority mode. He cannot simultaneously act as judge and defendant.
  • During the dark moon (absence of lunar light), his Lunar Decree power weakens significantly. Encounters timed to moonless nights operate at reduced celestial authority.

Keeper's Notes

Sariel is the entry in this codex that should make players most uncomfortable about their own characters. His power is not physical — it is revelatory. He does not fight you. He reads you. Every spiritual compromise a character has made, every quiet betrayal, every covenant whispered and then discarded — these are his intelligence report before a conversation begins. Use him to force players to reckon with what their characters have actually been doing, not just what they've been telling themselves they've been doing. A session with Sariel is a confession booth that grades on a celestial curve.
